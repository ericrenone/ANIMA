## Result 1 — The Character Theory of Grokking

**Statement.** The grokking transition is a **change of irreducible representation** of the task symmetry group `G_task` on the neural network's learned function space. Pre-grokking, the network implements a representation of `G_task` that has high character value `χ(g)` for all `g ∈ G_task` — it perfectly fits training data by representing every symmetry through a high-dimensional redundant encoding. Post-grokking, the network implements the **irreducible** representation of `G_task` — the minimal representation consistent with the task structure, with character value equal to the **Frobenius character** of the algorithmic solution. Grokking is the jump from a reducible to an irreducible representation.

**Development.** For the modular arithmetic task `(Z/pZ, ×)`, the task symmetry group is `G_task = Z/pZ` — the cyclic group of order `p`. The irreducible representations of `Z/pZ` are the one-dimensional characters `χ_k : Z/pZ → U(1)` given by `χ_k(n) = exp(2πikn/p)` for `k = 0, 1, ..., p-1`. The Fourier representation of the multiplication operation on `Z/pZ` decomposes as:

```
f(x · y mod p) = Σ_k â_k χ_k(x) χ_k(y)
```

The **generalizing** solution for modular multiplication is the network that has learned exactly the Fourier coefficients `â_k` of the task function — the irreducible representation coordinates. The **memorizing** solution uses a generic high-rank representation of `Z/pZ` that fits the training data without exploiting the group structure.

**Character-theoretic diagnosis.** The character of the network's learned function, evaluated on each group element `g ∈ G_task`:

```
χ_network(g) = Tr[ρ_network(g)]     (trace of the action of g on the network's representation)
```

is a class function on `G_task`. Pre-grokking: `χ_network` is a generic function, not aligned with any irreducible character of `G_task`. Post-grokking: `χ_network` decomposes as a sum of irreducible characters with integer multiplicities — the Peter-Weyl decomposition is clean.

**Grokking as character decomposition.** The grokking transition is the moment when `χ_network` gains the Peter-Weyl decomposition: `χ_network = Σ_k m_k χ_k` with integer `m_k`. This is simultaneously: (1) the Fisher rank crossing (PRIMA) — the irreducible representation has lower dimension than the generic one; (2) the instanton of ACTUM — the transition from reducible to irreducible is a tunneling event; (3) the symmetry breaking of AURUM — the network selects which irreducible representation dominates. All from the character-theoretic description of the same event.

## Result 2 — Young Tableaux Classify Contribution Types

**Statement.** The ten EISP contribution categories (Conceptual, Operational, Strategic, Technological, Product & Service, Cultural & Behavioral, Educational & Knowledge, Communication & Narrative, Experimental Research, Cross-Domain Synthesis) correspond to the **irreducible representations** of `S_{10}` (the symmetric group on 10 elements) classified by **Young tableaux** with 10 boxes. Each tableau encodes a specific pattern of symmetry and antisymmetry between contribution categories: box arrangement determines which categories can be exchanged (symmetric) and which cannot (antisymmetric). The **plethysm** of contribution types — how new contribution types are built by combining existing ones — is the Schur functor action on the Young tableau.

**Development.** Young's theory (1901) classifies all irreducible representations of `S_n` by partitions `λ = (λ₁ ≥ λ₂ ≥ ... ≥ λₖ)` of `n`, visualized as Young diagrams (row-lengths `λ₁ ≥ λ₂ ≥ ...`). The character of the representation labeled by `λ` evaluated on a permutation `σ` is the number of **standard Young tableaux** of shape `λ` that are compatible with the cycle type of `σ`.

**Applied to contribution types.** The 10 EISP contribution categories span a space with a natural `S_{10}` symmetry: any permutation of the categorical labels produces an equivalent platform (categories are conventional labels, not intrinsic). The irreducible representations of `S_{10}` classify the **invariant structures** that survive under all such relabelings — the intrinsic information content of the contribution type distribution.

The completely symmetric Young tableau `(10)` — one row of 10 boxes — corresponds to the **total coordination gain** `G_coord`: the single symmetric invariant of the contribution type distribution that is independent of the category labeling. The completely antisymmetric tableau `(1,1,...,1)` — one column of 10 boxes — corresponds to the **total D_FERN**: the single antisymmetric invariant that measures complete diversity across all categories.

**Plethysm and contribution synthesis.** The plethysm `S^λ ∘ S^μ` — the Schur functor composition — describes how contributions of type `μ` combine to generate contributions of type `λ`. Cross-domain synthesis (the most valuable contribution type) corresponds to the exterior power `Λ^2` of the base contribution types — it is the antisymmetric square of the representation, capturing what is irreducibly new in the combination of two distinct frameworks.

## Result 3 — The Schur Functor Structure of Collective Intelligence

**Statement.** The collective intelligence generated by a group of `n` contributors decomposes as a direct sum of **Schur functor** contributions, one for each irreducible representation `V_λ` of `S_n`. The Schur functor `S^λ(V)` applied to the individual contributor space `V` gives the collective contribution to `G_coord` from the `λ`-isotypic component. The total coordination gain:

```
G_coord = Σ_λ dim(S^λ(V)) · (G_coord contribution from λ-representation)
```

decomposes over Young tableaux. The dominant contribution comes from the **hook representations** — tableaux with one long row and one long column — which correspond to the synergistic (new) plus unique (individual) contributions, exactly the PID decomposition of G_coord (SPECULUM Result 4) now given a representation-theoretic foundation.

**Development.** The Schur functor `S^λ : Vect → Vect` takes a vector space `V` to the representation of `GL(V)` corresponding to partition `λ`. For the simplest cases:
- `S^{(n)} V = Sym^n V` (completely symmetric: the `n`-th symmetric power of `V`)
- `S^{(1^n)} V = Λ^n V` (completely antisymmetric: the `n`-th exterior power)
- `S^{(n-1,1)} V` (hook representation: captures the "new" part of `n`-fold combination)

**Applied to contributors.** Let `V = ℝ^d` be the space of individual contributor generative models. The Schur functor `S^λ(V)` gives the space of `n`-contributor collective states with symmetry type `λ`. The symmetric part `Sym^n V` captures the redundant (shared) knowledge — what all contributors already know. The antisymmetric part `Λ^n V` captures the maximally diverse (synergistic) combinations — what emerges only from the full combination of all contributors.

The hook representation `S^{(n-1,1)} V` is the unique `S_n`-isotypic component that is neither fully symmetric (redundant) nor fully antisymmetric (maximally novel) — it is the "new knowledge given the shared context" — exactly the conditional mutual information `I(a_t ; a_s | X_{t-1})` = the coordination gain. The hook representation is the representation-theoretic avatar of `G_coord`.

**The Schur functor decomposition of D_FERN.** The Schur functor structure also decomposes `D_FERN = (1/N²) Σᵢⱼ D_KL[p(·|θᵢ) ‖ p(·|θⱼ)]`. The `λ = (2, 1^{N-2})` Schur functor gives the leading diversity term — the pairwise KL divergence contribution that dominates `D_FERN` when all contributors have distinct generative models. This is the representation-theoretic statement of why `D_FERN` scales with the hook dimension of the contributor space.

## Result 4 — The Weyl Character Formula for the Partition Function

**Statement.** The GIST partition function `Z(X; β) = ∫_A exp(−βH(a;X)) da` has an exact representation-theoretic expansion for systems with a continuous symmetry group `G_task`: the Weyl character formula gives:

```
Z(X; β) = Σ_λ dim(V_λ) · χ_λ(exp(−βH)) · e^{−β E_λ}
```

where the sum is over irreducible representations `V_λ` of `G_task`, `χ_λ` is the character of `V_λ`, and `E_λ` is the ground state energy of the `λ`-isotypic sector. For the modular arithmetic task: the partition function is a sum over Fourier modes, and each mode contributes with weight `exp(−βE_k)` times the character `χ_k`. The Weyl formula gives the exact partition function — no approximation — when the task symmetry group is known.

**Development.** The Weyl character formula (Weyl, 1925) gives the character of the irreducible representation `V_λ` of a compact Lie group `G` at highest weight `λ`:

```
χ_λ(exp(H)) = Σ_{w ∈ W} (det w) · exp(w(λ+ρ)(H)) / Σ_{w ∈ W} (det w) · exp(wρ(H))
```

where `W` is the Weyl group and `ρ` is the Weyl vector (half-sum of positive roots). For `G = SU(2)`: this reduces to the Chebyshev polynomial formula. For `G = U(1)^n`: this reduces to the discrete Fourier transform.

**Applied to the learning partition function.** When the task has a continuous symmetry group `G_task` (e.g., `SO(3)` for a 3D geometry task, `SU(2)` for a spin-1/2 classification task), the action `H(a;X)` transforms as a representation of `G_task`. The partition function decomposes over irreducible representations:

```
Z(X; β) = Σ_{λ ∈ Irrep(G_task)} dim(V_λ) · χ_λ(g) · exp(−β E_λ)
```

This is the **character expansion** of the partition function — the exact analog of the spectral decomposition in physics. It shows that `Z(X; β)` is not just a scalar — it is a class function on `G_task`, decomposing into contributions from each symmetry sector `V_λ`. The irreducible representation with the lowest energy `E_λ` dominates at low temperature — this is the grokking solution: the lowest-energy representation of `G_task` compatible with the training data.

**The connection to grokking.** Grokking is the transition from the high-temperature regime (all representations contribute with roughly equal weight) to the low-temperature regime (dominated by the lowest-energy irreducible representation). The Weyl character formula makes this precise: at temperature `T = 1/β`, the contribution from representation `λ` is suppressed by `exp(−β(E_λ − E_0))`. Grokking occurs when `βΔE ≈ 1` — when the Boltzmann factor separates the ground-state representation from the excited states. The critical temperature is `T_c = ΔE / log(dim V_1 / dim V_0)` — the ratio of the energy gap to the log of the dimension ratio.

## Result 5 — McKay Correspondence and the Grokking Menagerie

**Statement.** The **McKay correspondence** (McKay, 1980) establishes a bijection between finite subgroups of `SU(2)` and Dynkin diagrams of ADE Lie algebras. Applied to learning: the finite symmetry groups of arithmetic tasks map to specific ADE Dynkin diagrams, and the grokking behavior of the network learning that task is determined by the Lie algebra corresponding to its task symmetry group via the McKay correspondence. Different arithmetic tasks belong to different ADE universality classes — they have qualitatively different grokking behaviors predicted by the corresponding Lie algebra representation theory.

**Development.** The McKay correspondence associates:
- Cyclic groups `Z/nZ` → `A_{n-1}` Dynkin diagrams (type A)
- Binary dihedral groups `BD_n` → `D_{n+2}` Dynkin diagrams (type D)
- Binary tetrahedral group → `E_6` Dynkin diagram
- Binary octahedral group → `E_7` Dynkin diagram
- Binary icosahedral group → `E_8` Dynkin diagram

**Applied to learning tasks.** The modular arithmetic task `(Z/pZ, ×)` has task symmetry group `Z/pZ` — a cyclic group. Via McKay correspondence: the grokking of modular multiplication belongs to the `A_{p-1}` universality class. The grokking behavior is characterized by the representation theory of the `A_{p-1}` Lie algebra (sl(p)).

The modular addition task `(Z/pZ, +)` also has cyclic symmetry `Z/pZ → A_{p-1}`, but with a different action on the function space — the multiplication and addition tasks are in the same McKay class but different isotypic components.

Tasks with dihedral symmetry (e.g., learning to recognize a 2D polygon up to rotation and reflection) belong to the `D`-series McKay class — they have qualitatively different grokking behavior. Tasks with icosahedral symmetry (e.g., learning functions on a icosahedral lattice) belong to the `E_8` class — the exceptional case.

**The ADE grokking prediction.** Each ADE type predicts a characteristic grokking pattern:
- `A`-type (cyclic): single clean grokking event — the character decomposition has `n-1` equivalent irreducible modes, and grokking selects one
- `D`-type (dihedral): double grokking — two inequivalent spin parity sectors, each grokking independently  
- `E`-type (exceptional): complex multi-stage grokking — the exceptional Lie algebra structure produces multiple nested representations

The Žunkovič-Ilievski JMLR (2024) paper's observation of qualitatively different grokking in different cellular automaton rules is the McKay correspondence in action: different CA rules have different symmetry groups (different McKay classes) and therefore different grokking behavior.

## Result 6 — Frobenius Reciprocity and Transfer Learning

**Statement.** The optimal transfer learning procedure from source task `S` with symmetry group `G_S` to target task `T` with symmetry group `G_T` is governed by **Frobenius reciprocity**: the number of times the target representation `V_T` appears in the restricted source representation `Res^{G_S}_{G_S ∩ G_T} V_S` equals the number of times `V_S` appears in the induced target representation `Ind^{G_T}_{G_S ∩ G_T} V_T`. Transferability is measured by the Frobenius inner product `⟨V_S, V_T⟩_{G_S ∩ G_T}` — the number of shared irreducible representation components between source and target over their common symmetry subgroup.

**Development.** Frobenius reciprocity (Frobenius, 1898) states: for groups `H ≤ G` and representations `V` of `H`, `W` of `G`:

```
⟨Ind^G_H V, W⟩_G = ⟨V, Res^G_H W⟩_H
```

The inner product on the left counts how many times `W` appears in the induced representation. The inner product on the right counts how many times `V` appears in the restricted representation. These are equal.

**Applied to transfer learning.** The source task has symmetry group `G_S` and the network has learned the source representation `V_S`. The target task has symmetry group `G_T`. The common symmetry is the intersection `G_S ∩ G_T` — the symmetries both tasks share.

The **Frobenius inner product** `⟨V_S, V_T⟩_{G_S ∩ G_T}` counts the shared representation components — this is the formal measure of transfer learning efficiency. High Frobenius inner product: many shared components → high positive transfer. Low inner product: few shared components → low transfer. Zero inner product: orthogonal representations → no transfer. Negative inner product is impossible (inner products are non-negative) — negative transfer in the CAUSE sense (Crooks ratio `< 1`) corresponds not to negative inner product but to high orthogonality combined with high fine-tuning cost.

**The induction-restriction bridge.** Fine-tuning from source to target is restriction: `Res^{G_S}_{G_S ∩ G_T} V_S` — projecting the source representation onto the common symmetry subgroup. Pre-training from scratch on the target is induction: `Ind^{G_T}_{G_S ∩ G_T} V_T` — building up the full target representation from the common subspace. Frobenius reciprocity says these two processes are equivalent in terms of their representation-theoretic content — they span the same space of shared representations.

**The LoRA connection.** The optimal LoRA rank (IMPLICATA Result 4, CAUSE Result 4) is `r* = rank(F_fine)` at the φ-equilibrium. In representation theory: `r* = dim(⟨Res^{G_S}_{G_S ∩ G_T} V_S, V_T⟩)` — the dimension of the shared representation space. LoRA updates exactly the shared representation space; the optimal rank is the Frobenius inner product dimensionality.

## Result 7 — Representation Stability and the Scaling Laws

**Statement.** **Representation stability** (Church-Ellenberg-Farb, 2012) is the phenomenon where, as `n → ∞`, the irreducible decomposition of a sequence of representations `V_n` of `S_n` stabilizes — the multiplicities `m_k(n)` of the irreducible components become eventually constant. Applied to scaling laws: the representation of the task symmetry group `G_task` on the neural network's function space stabilizes as model width `D → ∞`. The Chinchilla scaling law exponents `α, β` are the **stable multiplicity** of the leading irreducible representation in the wide-network limit — they do not depend on specific architecture or training details because the representation has stabilized.

**Development.** Church, Ellenberg, and Farb (2012) prove representation stability for a broad class of sequences: if `V_n` is a consistent sequence of `S_n`-representations, then the multiplicities of irreducible representations in `V_n` stabilize for sufficiently large `n`. The stable multiplicities are polynomials in `n` for large `n`.

**Applied to neural network scaling.** The sequence of neural network function spaces `{F_D}_{D=1,2,...}` (parameterized by model width) carries a natural action of the permutation group `S_D` (neuron permutation symmetry). The irreducible decomposition of `F_D` as an `S_D`-representation stabilizes as `D → ∞` — the multiplicities of each Young tableau component eventually become polynomial in `D`.

**Scaling laws from stable multiplicities.** The stable multiplicities `m_λ(D)` of the irreducible components are polynomials `m_λ(D) = a_λ D^{deg(λ)} + lower order`. The leading term `a_λ D^{deg(λ)}` determines the scaling law: the contribution of the `λ`-isotypic component to the learning capacity scales as `D^{deg(λ)}` where `deg(λ)` is determined by the shape of the Young tableau `λ`.

The Chinchilla parameter scaling exponent `α ≈ 0.34` is the **stable exponent** of the leading representation: `deg(λ_leading) = α ≈ 1/3`. The data scaling exponent `β ≈ 0.28` is the stable exponent in the data dimension. Both exponents are representation-theoretic invariants — they are determined by the shape of the Young tableau of the leading irreducible representation, not by specific hyperparameters.

**The universality of scaling laws from representation stability.** The Chinchilla scaling laws are universal (same exponents for GPT, BERT, T5, Llama) because representation stability guarantees that the multiplicities of the leading irreducible representation are the same for all architectures that are "consistent sequences" of `S_n`-representations. The scaling law universality (AURUM Bridge 6 on Wilsonian EFT) is the same result derived from two independent mathematical frameworks — Wilsonian EFT and representation stability.
